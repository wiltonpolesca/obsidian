**Explanation**

Prepositions are prevalent in English. Some of the most widely used prepositions are *of, to, in, on, at, by, with, for, from, up,* and* under*. Prepositions often occur in combination with certain nouns, verbs and adjectives.

What's your ***permission of*** the new buildings on State Street?Some people ***insist to*** the plan.I'm not ***opposed to*** the plan.Who is ***responsible for*** the money?
In the fist example, the noun *impression* is followed by *of.* In the second example, the verb *insist* is followed by the preposition *on.* In the third and fourthg examples, the adjectives *opposed* and *responsable* are  followed by *to* and* for,* respectively.

**Verbs**

Many verbs in English are followed by preposition. Some common examples are ***combine with, depend, on,*** and ***consist of****. *<u>***In some cases, memorization is the only way to learn these combinations***</u>. However, <u>sometimes it is helpful to group verbs with similar meanings</u>. For example, the meanings for *reply* and *depend* are, similar, and both are followed by on.

You can ***rely**** (depend) ****on*** Jason for advice.*Combine* and *mix* also hare a similar meaning. Both are followed by *with.*

***Combine**** (mix)* the eggs ***with*** the flour.

Other verbs such as *unite, put together, mingle,* and *blend* can added to this group

<u>Keep in mind that verbs with similar meanings may not be followed by the same preposition.</u>

Take, for instance, *insist on* and * demand.*

> He insisted on a clean coffee cup.
> I demanded a clean coffee cup. (no preposition)

Annotation: Todo verbo precedido de uma preposição vem com "ing"
