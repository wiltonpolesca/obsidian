5 -     The mistakes Dilma Rousseff made during her first presidential tem mean her second will be stormy

6 -

7 -     While Dilma Rousseff prepared to be sworn in for a second term as Brazil's president on January 1 st, the skies

8 - over the capital, Brasília, were forecast to be clear. But the outlook for the next four years is gloomy. Her daunting to-do

9 - list includes repairing ties with America, damaged by the revelation in 2013 that its spies had tapped her phone calls.

10 - Deforestation in the Amazon region is rising after a decade of decline, and the worst drought on record threatens to bring

11 - energy and water rationing to the industrial south-east. Preparations for the 2016 Olympics in Rio de Janeiro risk a reprise

12 - of the deadline- and budget-busting run-up to the 2014 football World Cup. which Brazil also hosted. Ms Rousseff's left-

13 - wing Workers' Party (PT) and its allies are embroiled in a corruption scandal involving Petrobras, the state-controlled oil

14 - giant, though so far she is personally untainted.

15 -     But it is the economy where the storm-clouds are stacked highest. The end of the commodity supercycle means

16 - falling prices for Brazilian exports of soyabeans, iron ore and, most recently, oil. And the policies Ms Rousseff pursued

17 - during their first term have proved disastrous. A combination of macroeconomic laxity and microeconomic meddling,

18 - intended to boost growth, merely undermined public finances and her credibility. GDP rose by just 6.7% during her first

19 - four years. Her biddable Central Bank governor, Alexandre Tombini, and finance minister, Guido Mantega, cut interest

20 - rates and let rip on public spending even as inflation rose and tax receipts slowed. If her second term is to be any better,

21 - she will need to undo much of what she did in the first.

22 -     Ms Rousseff has made a start by recruiting Joaquim Levy, a hawkish banker, to replace Mr. Mantega, and Nelson

23 - Barbosa, a respected economist, to the planning ministry, where he will oversee public investment. Mr. Tombini will

24 - remain at the Central Bank, but has clearly been told to take the inflation target of 4.5% seriously; since Ms Rousseff's

25 - victory in October, the benchmark interest rate has been raised from 11% to 11.17%. New agriculture and trade ministers

26 - with ties to farmers and industry signal a truce with the maligned private sector. The foreign ministry, too, is expected to

27 - get a more trade-friendly boss.

28 -     Mr. Levy, in particular, has his work cut on. He has promise a primary budget surplus (before interest payments)

29 - of 1.2% of GDP in 2015 and 2% in 2016 in order to avoid Brazil losing its investment-grade credit rating. But under Mr

30 - Mantega opaque and inefficient subsidies to energy, transport and credit ballooned. And half of all primary public

31 - spending (including on pensions) moves in step with the minimum wage, which is to rise bu around 2.5% in real terms in

32 - 2015 under a multi-year formula that links it to past GDP growth. This means that Mr. Levy must find savings of 2.1% of

33 - GDP elsewhere. A surplus of 0.7-0.8% is more plausible, thinks Mansueto Almeida, a public finance expert.

34 -     Even hitting hat lower target will mean cutting public investment and raising taxes - thereby making a return to

35 - growth even harder to achieve in the short term. Brazil's tax burden, already at 36% of GDP, is far higher than that of

36 - other middle-income countries., And its big construction firms, which are alleged to have bribed Petrobras for contracts,

37 - are likely to get caught up in legal proceedings and thus barred from public work. That puts at risk planned infrastructure

38 - projects budgeted at 870 billion reais ($325 billion), including some needed for the Olympics. After a 7.2% drop in

39 - investment in 2014, Itaú, a bank, expects investment to be flat in 2015. Analysts have duly slashed growth forecast for

40 - 2015 from 2.5% a year ago to 0.8% or less. Some predict an outright recession.

2 - the minimum wage should merely keep pace with inflation. Ms. Rousseff's, by contrast, is likely to become harder, thinks

3 - João Castro Neves of Eurasia Group, a consultancy. The PT's left-wingers and their sympathizers in trade unions and

4 - social movements despise Mr. Levy, whom they call "Scissorhands". The party's allies in government are in a mutinous

5 - mood: in December 35 of 781 congressmen from its biggest coalition partner refused to vote with the government to revise

6 - this year's unattainable primary-surplus target of 1.9% (though the measure passed anyway). The Petrobras afair, which

7 - the opposition is exploiting with gusto, will further deplete the president's already diminished political capital.

8 -     Austerity will also hit her popularity in the country at large. Petrol prices have already gone up; electricity and

9 - public transporte are next. The most recent plan to raise bus fares in big cities, in June 2013, sparked the biggest protests in

10 - a generation, and was quickly dropped. Any fiscal and monetary adjustment big enough to restore public finances is sure

11 - to push up the jobless rate, which is now close to a record low of around 5%.

12 -     Ideally, Ms Rousseff will let Mr. Levy snip way, and use the Petrobras scandal to revitalize the ailing oil and

13 - construction industries by opening them up to foreign competition and dropping onerous (and graft-inducing) local-

14 - content rules. But having promised Brazilians that belt-tightening would be painless, she may unbuckle at the first twinges

15 - of discomfort. Even if she does not, her new-found appetite for reforms will not be matched by her capacity to accomplish

16 - them.
